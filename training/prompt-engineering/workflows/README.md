# Why This Training Exists

Insider Threat has always been a human problem.

Technology changes, but organizations continue to face the same challenge:

**How do we identify and reduce risk created by trusted individuals while enabling the business to succeed?**

Throughout my career, I've had the opportunity to help stand up two Insider Threat programs.

Although every organization was different, I found myself encountering many of the same questions over and over again.

- Where do we start?
- What does a successful Insider Threat program actually look like?
- Which stakeholders need to be involved?
- What technologies should we invest in?
- How do we build trust with HR, Legal, Privacy, and leadership?
- How do we investigate consistently, ethically, and defensibly?
- How do we mature beyond simply responding to alerts?

This training exists because I wanted to capture the lessons learned from those experiences and share them with others building their own programs.

Not vendor documentation.

Not marketing.

Practical guidance from real-world experience.

# Philosophy

Technology does not build an Insider Threat program.

People do.

Successful programs are built on:

- Executive sponsorship
- Governance
- Cross-functional partnerships
- Clearly defined policies
- Repeatable investigative processes
- Trust
- Continuous improvement

Technology simply enables those capabilities.

Microsoft Purview, Microsoft Graph, PowerShell, Python, Artificial Intelligence.

Each is a tool. None replaces good investigators.

# Lessons from the Field

Over the years, several lessons have consistently proven themselves regardless of organization or technology.

## Insider Threats Are Not Always Malicious

One of the biggest misconceptions about Insider Threat is that every investigation involves someone intentionally trying to harm the organization.

Many investigations involve well-intentioned employees making poor decisions, violating policy, or creating risk without malicious intent.

An effective Insider Threat program focuses on understanding **risk**, not assigning blame.

---

## Learn What "Normal" Looks Like

You cannot recognize abnormal behavior until you understand what normal behavior looks like in your own environment.

Every organization operates differently.

Understanding that baseline is often more valuable than the alert itself.

Context matters.

---

## Think Outside the Box

Successful investigations rarely follow a checklist.

Investigators need to think creatively, correlate information across multiple systems, and develop hypotheses based on evidence.

However...

Creativity should never come at the expense of governance.

Think outside the box while remaining firmly inside legal, privacy, and organizational boundaries.

---

## Data Governance Is Security

Your data cannot be protected—or investigated properly—if it is not understood.

Classification, Labeling, Retention, Ownership.

These are not simply compliance exercises. They are foundational capabilities that enable effective Insider Threat investigations.

---

## Every Investigation Needs a Purpose

Modern security platforms make it incredibly easy to collect data.

That does not mean every investigation should begin with broad searches or eDiscovery.

Every investigative action should have:

- A documented business purpose
- Appropriate scope
- Organizational approval when required
- Respect for employee privacy
- Alignment with policy

Trust with HR, Legal, Privacy, Compliance, and leadership is difficult to build and incredibly easy to lose.

Every investigative action should be one you can confidently explain, document, and justify.

Investigate with purpose.

# Why AI?

Artificial intelligence is the latest technological evolution that security professionals must understand.

Like the internet before it, AI creates new opportunities for organizations, new opportunities for defenders, and new opportunities for adversaries.

Ignoring AI is not a strategy.

Understanding it is.

Throughout this toolkit, AI is presented as a force multiplier for investigators—not a replacement for them.

Examples include:

- Organizing investigative information
- Summarizing evidence
- Drafting reports
- Identifying relationships
- Assisting with policy analysis
- Automating repetitive documentation
- Supporting investigative decision-making

The objective is not to remove humans from the investigative process.

The objective is to allow investigators to spend less time documenting and more time investigating.

Human judgment remains the most valuable capability in any Insider Threat program.
