

## Lessons from the Field

Building an effective Insider Threat program requires far more than deploying technology. While every organization is different, there are several lessons that consistently prove valuable in real-world operations.

## Insider Threats Are Not Always Malicious

One of the biggest misconceptions about Insider Threat is that every case involves a malicious employee.

In reality, many investigations involve individuals who had no intent to cause harm. Well-intentioned employees can still create significant risk through negligence, misunderstanding, policy violations, or attempts to work around security controls to "get the job done."

An effective Insider Threat program focuses on understanding **risk**, not simply assigning blame.

---

## Understand What "Normal" Looks Like

You cannot identify abnormal behavior until you understand normal behavior.

Every organization has unique workflows, business processes, technologies, and cultural norms. Activities that appear suspicious in one environment may be completely expected in another.

Successful Insider Threat programs invest time in understanding:

- Typical user behavior
- Department-specific workflows
- Administrative activities
- Data movement patterns
- Seasonal business changes
- Operational exceptions

Context is often just as important as the alert itself.

---

## Think Creatively—Operate Responsibly

Insider Threat investigations rarely follow a checklist.

Investigators often need to think creatively, correlate information across multiple systems, and identify relationships that are not immediately obvious.

However, creativity should never come at the expense of governance.

Every investigative action should remain consistent with organizational policies, legal requirements, privacy expectations, and documented investigative procedures.

Good investigators think outside the box while remaining firmly inside legal and ethical boundaries.

---

## Data Governance Matters

Technology can only protect information that the organization understands.

Without proper data classification, labeling, retention, and governance, security teams lose valuable context during investigations and increase organizational risk.

Strong Insider Threat programs work closely with data owners and governance teams to ensure information is:

- Properly classified
- Appropriately retained
- Protected according to business requirements
- Accessible when legitimate investigations require it

Data governance is a foundational capability—not an afterthought.

---

## Investigate with Purpose

Modern investigation platforms make it technically easy to search large amounts of organizational data.

That does **not** mean every investigation should begin with broad searches or eDiscovery.

Every investigative action should have a documented business justification, an appropriate scope, and stakeholder alignment.

Launching unnecessary or poorly justified eDiscovery searches can create avoidable friction with Human Resources, Legal, Privacy, Compliance, and other organizational partners.

Successful Insider Threat programs build trust by demonstrating that investigations are:

- Purpose-driven
- Well documented
- Proportionate to the identified risk
- Consistent with organizational policy

The goal is not to collect as much data as possible.

The goal is to collect the **right** data to answer the investigative question.
